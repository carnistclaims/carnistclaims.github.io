Claim: I base my morality on a species-by-species basis

Details

The carnist here claims that variously: - moral arguments should be made by species - moral arguments are granted by species because members of a species can breed to make more of that species

Problems with this Argument

1. Species isn't even a single thing

This argument by Stefan Molyneux was presented in terms of animals that give birth to other animals. Well sorry to say, but all animals have a common ancestor, so by Molyneux's own definition he should be a vegan. The problem with using 'species' is that boundaries on species are fuzzy, is a ring species a single 'species' by this definition? So this is already a problematic and fuzzy area, and if you think that fuzzy boundaries are no problem, remember we're talking about a dividing line as harsh as 'you go to jail for killing species X' and 'you go to jail for stopping someone from killing species Y'.

2. Species is an arbitrary classification

You choose species. Cool, I choose skin color... or race... or gender. Wait, what makes species a better dividing line? Every single answer ever given has been name-the-trait can-kicking at best. E.g. "Oh, it's because X", wherein X is simply part of the arbitrary definition of species. It never actually gets clarified.

3. Carnists don't even believe their own argument

Ask a carnist if they'd eat a golden retriever and they say no. But coyote hunting is a thing, and most carnists won't object to it, even though coyotes, wolves, and dogs are all the same species and can interbreed with each other. And there are plenty of cases where carnists don't grant different members of our own species the same rights. So their own argument isn't even followed or truly believed by carnists anyway.

4. Morality by class

Why should the morality of individual A towards individual X be dictated by the existence or characteristics of individual Y or W that are not part of this transaction? Is there any other (less morally convenient) example wherein that's the case (ignoring emergent properties - we're not talking about you are part of species X and therefore have characteristic Y, and the real criterion is characteristic Y - we're talking about being a part of species X itself being a criterion for morality)? E.g. why is the morality of whether or not I get to kill you based on whether or not there are a bunch of other things that look like you around such that I bunch you all in together and give you all the same (inferior to my own) rights? It's a nonsensical argument.

5. Collateral damage

This argument still doesn't take into account the ecological and human impact of eating meat, and it remains unethical for those reasons as well.
